# Frontier Vision

## What is Frontier?

Frontier is a survival RPG about curiosity, discovery, and the slow rebuilding of civilization.

Players begin with almost nothing. Through observation, experimentation, and persistence, they learn how to survive in an unfamiliar world.

The game is not about becoming a legendary hero.

It is about becoming another chapter in the world's history.

---

# The Player Experience

Players should feel like they are:

* exploring an unknown wilderness;
* learning through experience;
* making meaningful discoveries;
* leaving permanent marks on the world;
* contributing to something larger than themselves.

Progress should feel earned rather than granted.

---

# Core Fantasy

"I don't know what's over that hill."

Exploration should always create curiosity.

The next location, discovery, landmark, or story should feel worth pursuing.

---

# The Role of Knowledge

Knowledge is the primary progression system.

The player does not simply become stronger.

They become wiser.

Understanding the world unlocks new opportunities.

Learning should be rewarded more often than grinding.

---

# The World

The world should feel ancient.

Nature constantly reclaims what civilization leaves behind.

Most places have history.

Many stories have been forgotten.

Players slowly uncover fragments of that history.

---

# The Survivor

Every player begins as an ordinary survivor.

No chosen one.

No prophecy.

No destiny.

Survival itself is an accomplishment.

---

# Legacy

Survivors are temporary.

Civilizations endure.

History remembers only fragments.

A player's actions should eventually become part of the world's history.

---

# The First Survivor

Finnley is the First Survivor.

He was the first survivor ever created during Frontier's development.

Within the world, he exists as a historical figure whose life has largely faded into myth.

Players discover his story gradually through forgotten camps, journals, tools, landmarks, and oral traditions.

Finnley is never encountered directly.

His legacy exists through the world he helped shape.

---

# Emergent Storytelling

The best stories are not scripted.

They emerge naturally from gameplay.

Players should frequently finish a session with a story they created themselves.

The game should encourage those moments rather than replace them with cutscenes.

---

# Atmosphere

Quiet moments matter.

A ruined campsite.

A distant storm.

A journal entry.

A trail disappearing into the trees.

These moments should carry as much emotional weight as major gameplay systems.

---

# Design Philosophy

Whenever possible:

* choose systems over scripted events;
* choose player curiosity over explicit objectives;
* choose permanent consequences over temporary rewards;
* choose worldbuilding over exposition;
* choose exploration over repetition.

---

# Technical Philosophy

Content should be data-driven.

Adding a new biome should primarily involve creating resources rather than modifying gameplay code.

The engine should empower designers rather than restrict them.

---

# Community

Tester feedback is an important part of Frontier's development.

Suggestions should be evaluated against this vision.

Features that conflict with the project's identity should be reconsidered, even if they are popular.

---

# What Frontier Is Not

Frontier is not intended to be:

* a fast-paced action game;
* a theme-park quest RPG;
* a loot treadmill;
* a power fantasy;
* a game where progression is driven primarily by combat.

Combat may exist.

It is not the purpose of the experience.

---

# Guiding Question

Whenever a design decision is difficult, ask:

> "Does this make the player more curious about the world?"

If the answer is yes, it probably belongs in Frontier.
